Iteration · five visual commitments

Five ways to make § Exhibition a section, not a press release.

The exhibition was a one-day group show in Schöneberg on March 27, 2026 — six artists, three days, prints and code-driven installations side by side. The brief: surface that on the landing page without overpromising a recurring program. Pink discipline holds — badge, CTA, hover only, the way § 3 Workshops uses it.

Each option commits to a different visual idea. A reads as a sibling of § 3. B lets the poster carry the section. C splits by medium. D renders the show as a museum table. E draws the room. None of these are skins of the same skeleton — pick one (or a hybrid) and the live port begins.

What this commits to

Visual consistency with § 3. The "specimen" vocabulary, the dimmed-poster aside, the archive-head bar — all carry over. Adding Exhibition 02 later is one row.

The plate strip handles the "two registers" challenge by mixing print and animated thumbnails in one row; the "▶ live" marker keeps the medium distinction legible.

The plate strip is tight (5 cells); curating it across 20+ works will hurt.

What this commits to

The medium register is the organising idea. The page makes the print/animated distinction explicit and resolves the "two kinds of work" challenge in layout, not just in captions.

The animated half is honest about what's a finished capture and what's a stub. Hover affordance is consistent across all three pieces.

What it gives up

Foregrounds medium over the group-show frame. The six-artist roster lives in the banner meta and the captions — easy to miss at a glance.

Doesn't scale: a second exhibition's diptych would need either a second diptych below (heavy), or a tabbed switcher (chrome).

Option D Museum catalog table · mono-heavy · scalable

Specimen sheet · taxonomy table.

The exhibition rendered as a rigid museum-catalog table: each row is a work, columns are plate, title, artist, technique, format, and link. Mono-typography in the header, serif in the rows, small thumbnails in the plate column. The poster floats next to the section head as a small dimmed thumbnail. Cold, archival, and scales unmodified to 20 works or 200.

What this commits to

An archival, museum-catalog register. The exhibition reads as a documented event, not an upcoming spectacle. Scales unmodified to any number of works.

Treats prints and animated pieces symmetrically — they're rows of the same table, differentiated only by the Format column and the play marker on the plate.

What it gives up

Dry on first read. The table demands you scan before anything inviting arrives. The mandala poster shrinks to a thumbnail — the strongest visual asset is sidelined.

Sits uneasily between § 3 Workshops (warm catalog) and § 5 Research (research-quiet) — it doesn't borrow from either, which can make it feel like a third visual register on one page.

Leans hard into the "office → gallery" line from the poster. The section is itself a small artifact — memorable, photographable, distinct from everything else on the landing page.

Five visual commitments, compared.

One pitch and one risk per option. Hybrids are allowed — most likely candidates are A's specimen scaffold with C's diptych body inside it, or B's poster hero with D's table below.
